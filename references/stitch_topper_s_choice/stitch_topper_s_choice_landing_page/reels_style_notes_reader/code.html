Page 14: Theories of Comparative Advantage

The Ricardian Model

David Ricardo's theory suggests that international trade can benefit all participating nations if they specialize in producing goods for which they have a lower opportunity cost.

"Even if one nation is less efficient than another in the production of all commodities, there is still a basis for mutually beneficial trade."

Page 16: Modern Application

Case Study: The Silicon Shield

How Taiwan leverages specific comparative advantage in semiconductor fabrication to define global supply chains.

The concentration of advanced logic chip manufacturing in a single geographic location presents a unique intersection of economic theory and geopolitical reality.

By achieving economies of scale that no other nation can replicate, the 'Silicon Shield' demonstrates that comparative advantage isn't just about resource endowment, but also long-term institutional investment.

Critics argue that such hyper-specialization creates systemic vulnerabilities. However, the Ricardo model suggests that the global efficiency gain outweighs the localized risk factor.
